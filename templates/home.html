What Actually Predicts Your Marathon Time?

A machine learning analysis of 22,000+ marathon training cycles, built by Shih-An Shzu — runner and data scientist.

Run slow runs slow. Run fast runs fast.

Every runner hears this — and the model confirms it matters. Pace differentiation — the gap between your easy-run pace and your fast-run pace — is one of the strongest independent predictors of finish time. Runners who keep their easy runs easy and their hard runs hard finish faster than runners who train at the same pace every day, even at the same weekly mileage.

The model captures this through pace_diff and pace_ratio — both peak in importance around weeks 10–11 of training (peak fitness, 5–6 weeks before race day).

Your baseline fitness going in matters more than your peak week.

The model weights your early-training paces (weeks 1–4) heavier than your peak-week mileage. This isn't training advice — it's a reality check: the fitness you bring into the 16-week block is a stronger predictor than how hard you push during it.

Your long run and easy run paces reflect your aerobic base — they're your slowest runs of the week, and they should be. A runner whose easy pace is 8:00/mi is simply fitter than one at 10:30/mi. The training plan can refine that base, but it can't replace it.

The key takeaway

The model sees two distinct things: (1) your absolute fitness level (captured by your actual paces — not something a training plan can fake) and (2) the quality of your training structure (captured by pace differentiation — something you can control). Volume and frequency matter, but they're secondary.

The Tools

Finish Time Predictor

Upload 16 weeks of training data (CSV) and the Ridge model predicts your marathon finish time. RMSE ≈ 29 min on held-out data.

Training Schedule Generator

Enter a goal time and get a 16-week plan derived from the median training patterns of real runners who finished near your target — filtered by gender and age group.

About This Project

Built on Garmin/Strava training logs (2019–2020) covering 22,000+ marathon training cycles. The model is Ridge Regression (α = 51.79) with 157 features — per-week mileage, pace, long-run and fastest-run metrics across 16 weeks, plus demographics. Athlete-grouped cross-validation prevents data leakage between a runner's training history and their race result.

Tech: Python, scikit-learn, pandas, Flask.
